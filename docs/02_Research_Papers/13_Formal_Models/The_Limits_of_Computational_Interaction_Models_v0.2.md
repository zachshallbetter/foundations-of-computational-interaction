# The Limits of Computational Interaction Models

**Version:** Draft 0.2  
**Project:** Computational Interaction Science  
**Status:** Foundational Position Paper

---

# Abstract

Every scientific framework is defined not only by what it explains, but by what it refuses to claim.

This document establishes the epistemic boundaries for Computational Interaction Science. It argues that interaction models should seek structure without determinism, formalization without false certainty, generality without reductionism, and integration without replacing adjacent disciplines.

The purpose of these limits is not to weaken the discipline. It is to make its claims scientifically defensible.

---

# 1. Why Boundaries Matter

New disciplines often fail by overextending their explanatory authority.

If Computational Interaction Science attempts to explain culture, cognition, personality, intelligence, organizations, and society through interaction alone, it will become another grand unified theory with little empirical value.

Instead, the discipline should explicitly define where its authority begins and ends.

Interaction models explain interaction.

They do not explain people in their entirety.

---

# 2. Culture Does Not Determine Behavior

Culture shapes exposure, meaning, expectations, conventions, symbolism, and interaction practices.

It does not determine behavior.

The preferred model is:

```text
Cultural and community context
        ↓
changes probabilities of exposure,
meaning, expectation, and practice
        ↓
interacts with individual history,
task, representation, and situation
        ↓
contributes to behavior
```

Research should therefore investigate mechanisms rather than treating demographic identity as causal.

---

# 3. Interaction History Does Not Fully Explain Individuals

Accumulated interaction experience may improve explanation beyond demographic categories, but it is only one explanatory layer.

Behavior also depends upon:

- current objectives;
- domain knowledge;
- accessibility;
- fatigue;
- immediate context;
- consequences;
- uncertainty;
- individual variation.

Interaction Ecology should therefore remain probabilistic rather than deterministic.

```text
Behavior =
interaction history
× task
× representation
× opportunity
× immediate state
× individual variation
```

---

# 4. Interaction May Not Reduce to a Fixed Grammar

A canonical interaction grammar is valuable only if it preserves explanatory power.

Interaction may instead require a layered grammar.

```text
Input Form
↓

Semantic Action
↓

Situated Action
↓

Activity
```

The discipline should determine whether interaction possesses a finite primitive grammar, an extensible grammar, or no stable grammar at all.

---

# 5. Internal Cognition Cannot Be Directly Observed

Interaction systems observe behavior.

They do not observe beliefs, confidence, curiosity, understanding, trust, or intention directly.

Those remain latent constructs.

Every inference should preserve the distinction:

```text
Observed Behavior
        ↓
Behavioral Feature
        ↓
Candidate Interpretation
        ↓
Uncertainty
```

Behavior must never be silently promoted into psychology.

---

# 6. Representation Is Not the Only Cause of Behavior

Representation influences interaction.

It does not fully explain it.

Behavior emerges through multiple interacting factors.

```text
Behavior =
semantic state
× representation
× morphology
× interaction grammar
× opportunity structure
× agent state
× task
```

Representation should therefore be studied as one manipulated variable rather than the sole explanatory mechanism.

---

# 7. Formalization Does Not Eliminate Ambiguity

Ontologies, formal models, and computational systems improve reproducibility.

They do not eliminate ambiguity.

Formalization moves ambiguity into:

- construct definitions;
- event classification;
- opportunity boundaries;
- model assumptions;
- interpretation rules.

The contribution of formalization is not certainty.

Its contribution is inspectable uncertainty.

---

# 8. One Ontology Should Not Replace Existing Disciplines

Computational Interaction Science should integrate findings across:

- Human–Computer Interaction;
- Psychology;
- Anthropology;
- Human Factors;
- Design;
- Information Science.

It should not replace them.

The ontology provides a shared relational structure through which findings become comparable.

---


# 9. Similarity Is Not a Universal Score

Interaction comparison is always relative to a target, frame, correspondence mapping, observables, admissible transformations, metrics, tolerances, uncertainty, and validity domain.

Semantic similarity does not establish opportunity, behavioral, strategy, causal, or measurement similarity. When a defensible common comparison space cannot be established, the result is incomparability rather than a forced ranking.

# 10. A Trace Does Not Reveal a Strategy

Strategies are latent explanatory constructs. A repeated sequence supports a descriptive regularity, not by itself a plan, policy, intention, habit, or strategy.

Scientific strategy attribution requires rival models, diagnostic contingencies, uncertainty, repeated evidence, and preferably intervention or out-of-sample discrimination.

# 11. A Metric Is Not a Construct

A numerical measure does not become comprehension, confidence, expertise, trust, quality, or accessibility merely by being renamed. Every measurement claim must declare its measurand, observation process, operationalization, scale, uncertainty, validity evidence, population, task, projection, and permitted interpretation.

# 12. Contracts Do Not Eliminate Judgment

Projection, Opportunity, Interaction, Behavioral Evidence, Measurement, Similarity, and Strategy Contracts make assumptions inspectable. They do not eliminate boundary choices, coding judgment, model selection, interpretation, or uncertainty.

# 13. A Doctrine of Bounded Explanation

Computational Interaction Science should adopt the following doctrine:

- Seek structure without determinism.
- Seek formalization without false certainty.
- Seek generality without reductionism.
- Seek integration without disciplinary replacement.
- Preserve uncertainty rather than concealing it.
- Distinguish observation from interpretation.
- Prefer mechanisms over categories.

These principles define the epistemic limits of the discipline.

---

# 14. Conclusion

The long-term credibility of Computational Interaction Science will depend as much on the claims it refuses to make as the claims it advances.

A disciplined ontology should clarify what interaction models can explain, what they may only suggest, and what remains outside their authority.

Those boundaries are not weaknesses.

They are prerequisites for scientific rigor.
