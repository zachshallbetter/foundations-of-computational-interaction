# The Interface Is a Field, Not a Screen

## The Question Was Never Just Visual

The question began while I was building an artist directory representing Indigenous artists from communities across North America. I had already designed many tribe-specific interfaces and had seen how dramatically their visual and narrative languages could differ. It was tempting to ask whether heritage itself changed how people understood an interface.

That framing turned out to be too simple.

Culture matters, but it cannot be treated as a direct explanation of behavior. Heritage does not mechanically determine whether someone prefers a timeline, understands a hierarchy, trusts a badge, or recognizes an action. People participate in several communities at once. They accumulate experience through family, work, education, language, disability, games, institutions, and professional tools.

The more defensible question became whether sustained participation in a community produces a learned interaction language. A video editor reasons through tracks, clips, and playheads. A developer reasons through files, branches, and diffs. A designer reasons through spatial relationships, grouping, and direct manipulation. These are not merely preferences. They are learned structures for thinking.

That shift changed the research. The problem was no longer finding a universal interface. It became understanding how different representations preserve—or obscure—the same underlying reality.

## We Learned to Represent Structure

Modern computing has become extraordinarily good at representing structure.

HTML describes structural semantics. JSON describes data shape. Graphs describe topology. Components describe reusable units. Events describe occurrence. Accessibility standards describe roles, names, and states. Across nearly every layer of software, we have learned how to tell machines what something is.

What we have not learned to represent is why it matters.

A button may be correctly marked up, focusable, labeled, and connected to an event handler. None of that tells us why the action matters in the current workflow, what it enables, what it prevents, what evidence led to it, or whether the person using it understands the consequence.

A citation may be linked to a source without the system knowing how strongly that source supports the claim. A warning may be announced accessibly without the surrounding interface understanding what has become unstable or what recovery paths remain available.

When systems need to communicate urgency, confidence, validation, authority, priority, or trust, they usually fall back to conventions: color, badges, alerts, progress indicators, disabled controls, motion, typography, and layout. These conventions are effective for people because we learn them socially. They are not computational models of the ideas they represent.

The limitation is not that our standards are inadequate. They were designed to solve narrower problems. They faithfully describe structure. They do not preserve the relationships that give structure meaning.

The history of computing has largely been about teaching machines what things are. The next chapter may be about preserving why those things matter.
